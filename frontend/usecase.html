01 Why this matters for India, right now

Every UPI transaction, every Aadhaar e-KYC, every DigiLocker handshake, every NPCI settlement, every BFSI login is signed and encrypted today with cryptography that a future quantum computer will defeat. The window is ten to fifteen years. Foreign intelligence services are already recording the encrypted traffic. They cannot read it today. They expect to read it after 2032.

The shift is starting in India. MeitY, CERT-In, and RBI have flagged cryptographic agility as a forward security priority; the DPDP Act 2023 sharpens the cost of any credential-leak incident; an Indian PQC timetable is expected to follow the NSA / NIST wave already published abroad. Microsoft, Okta, Google have not yet shipped a generally-available answer for identity. India has a two-to-four-year head start to choose a sovereign one.

“Anything India encrypts today with the old locks is, for all practical purposes, public on a delay.”

Made-in-India is not a slogan here. It is the procurement criterion. A US or EU vendor cannot be empanelled for classified government workloads. A sovereign-cloud reseller cannot bundle a foreign identity layer. India 2047 needs an Indian-built, DPDP-aligned, CERT-In-ready, MeitY-empanelment-ready identity stack. That is what MILNET is.

02 Four Indian stories of what could go wrong
A government hospital network

AIIMS records, exposed in 2035

Encrypt patient records in 2026 with standard cryptography. An adversary copies the backups year after year. In 2035, when quantum computers are large enough, the entire archive is decrypted in a few weekends. HIV status, mental-health records, genetic markers, all on the open internet. The hospital cannot un-publish this.

An Indian bank or NPCI

One stolen key, every customer impersonated

Most identity systems sign login tokens with a single master key. Steal it, and you can mint logins for any customer on any service. Our system splits the key across five servers in three Indian data centres. An attacker has to break into three at once. Cracking one machine gets nothing.

A national identity programme

A subtle bug nobody finds for nine years

National identity software has bugs. Most are caught by testing. A few are not. We did the maths instead. Nineteen statements like “no expired token can ever be accepted” are mathematically proved impossible to violate. Aerospace and reactor software is written this way. Identity software is not. We thought it should be.

A trust authority (CCA / CA)

Quietly issuing fakes for two months

What if the trust authority itself is compromised? Every credential change in our system is published to a public ledger any auditor or paranoid customer can independently check. If something is issued that should not have been, it shows up within minutes, not weeks.

05 Four questions every Indian buyer should ask any identity vendor in 2026
01

Are you post-quantum today, or only on roadmap?

Demand a list of NIST FIPS 203 / 204 / 205 algorithms actually deployed in your identity tokens. Roadmap is not deployment. Roadmap means no.

02

If a single server is compromised, can the attacker mint tokens?

If the answer involves one master key, walk away. Threshold signing means three of five servers must agree before any token is signed.

03

Show me the formal proof that an expired token cannot be accepted.

Either there is a TLA+ / Coq / F* model with that exact statement, or there is not. There is no middle answer. Testing is not proof.

04

Where can I audit, daily, that you have not silently changed cryptographic keys?

The right answer is a public Merkle log URL, checked by an independent auditor. Not “trust us.” Not “we will tell you at incident response.”
